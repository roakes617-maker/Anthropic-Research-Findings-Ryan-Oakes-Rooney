Three Goals I Set on Day One

When I inherited this account in April 2020, the relationship was at rock bottom. The previous team had destroyed trust with the CIO by running an "Illuminate" engagement as a vehicle to force a multi-million dollar expansion. I set three goals:

✓Win Flex back as a CRM customer — replace SugarCRM with Salesforce Manufacturing Cloud

About Flex

Flex Ltd. is a $26B global manufacturing and supply chain company headquartered in Austin, Texas. With over 160,000 employees across 100+ facilities in 30+ countries, Flex is one of the world's largest diversified manufacturers — designing, building, and delivering products for some of the biggest brands in automotive, healthcare, industrial, cloud, and consumer electronics. Their scale and complexity made this one of the most strategically significant accounts in the territory.

Phase 1 — April 2020
Inheriting the Account at Rock Bottom
Inherited Flex after the previous team was kicked out entirely. Flex had asked Salesforce for help with an over-customized deployment and low adoption. Instead of addressing the root cause, Salesforce ran an "Illuminate" engagement — interviewing dozens of people — then used the findings to try and sell more technology. This destroyed trust with CIO Gus Shahin, who controls all technology decisions. As a result, Flex moved their CRM to SugarCRM, causing $1M in attrition. What remained: a small Service Cloud footprint for RMA/spare parts in Global Services, plus Mulesoft and Tableau.

Complex enterprise deals don't close because of one person. They close because the right people are mobilized at the right time, grounded in the right context, and aligned on the right outcome.

My Philosophy

Most AEs introduce the customer to a specialist and then disappear. I do the opposite. I stay in every room, personally ground every resource in the customer's specific business context, and make sure every internal partner understands not just what we're presenting — but why it matters to this customer. The account executive is the connective tissue between every function. If I'm not adding context, I'm not adding value.

This isn't just a deal-time behavior. I build relationships with product, engineering, success, and partner teams continuously — so when a deal requires mobilization, I've already earned the trust to ask for it.

Working with Product
Translating Customer Feedback Into Roadmap Influence
At Salesforce, I worked directly with the Sales Cloud and Manufacturing Cloud product organizations during the Flex deal. When Flex raised concerns about native Excel and Outlook integration — core Microsoft Dynamics arguments — I didn't just relay the objection. I brought product teams into the conversation, walked them through Flex's specific workflows, and helped them understand why these integrations mattered for a manufacturing seller managing 30,000 monthly change orders. The result: product built competitive positioning specifically addressing these gaps, and I fed back Flex's real-world requirements to inform the Manufacturing Cloud roadmap.
At Anthropic: I'd bring the same approach — connecting customer use cases directly to product teams. When a customer hits a limitation, I don't just log a ticket. I provide the business context that helps product prioritize.
Working with Technical Teams
Grounding Every Demo in Business Context
For the Flex SIC session, I worked with two Solution Engineers — Tye Jones and Katie Green, plus Michelle Collins on vision narratives and a dedicated video production team. I didn't hand them a brief and walk away. I sat with them through every demo build, every slide, every video script, making sure each capability vignette was grounded in Flex's actual operations: their quoting cycles, their change order volume, their global manufacturing footprint. When we presented at the SIC, I personally introduced every Flex attendee and explained how they mapped to the relationship. The SE team later said they'd never had an AE stay that involved.
At Anthropic: Whether it's a Solutions Architect building a proof of concept or an engineer scoping an API integration, I'd bring the same depth of customer context so the technical team can build something that actually solves the customer's problem.
Executive & Stakeholder Alignment
Orchestrating 5 Levels of Leadership
The Flex deal required alignment across my entire chain of command — from my RVP through the AVP, SVP, and CRO. Each exec needed different context at different moments. I brought in Rich DeGraff (SVP Success, 16-year Honeywell veteran) for manufacturing credibility with Flex's operations team. I identified that Justice Reid knew Cameron Carr from his Microsoft days and orchestrated a LinkedIn re-engagement to neutralize Cameron's Dynamics bias. At Anthropic: I'd map Anthropic's executive team to customer leadership early and build those relationships before a deal requires them. When the moment comes, the ask isn't cold — it's earned.

Navigating Product Limitations
When the Product Can't Do What the Customer Needs
During the Flex RFP, Microsoft's strongest argument was native integration with Excel, Outlook, and Teams, tools Flex's sellers lived in daily. Rather than conceding these points, I addressed each one head-on:
1. Challenged the use case — for Excel, I pushed back on how often this was actually needed in their day-to-day workflows. When they confirmed it was a real requirement, I showed AppExchange partners who had built these integrations
2. Proved Outlook parity — showed native Salesforce integrations with Outlook and pointed out that 90% of Salesforce's customer base were Microsoft Office and Outlook customers. This wasn't a gap — it was a solved problem
3. Built the Teams integration into the demo — instead of talking about it theoretically, I worked with the SE team to show parity live in the custom demo
4. Went beyond the RFP — didn't just answer their requirements, I showed the vision for what else was possible. Moved the conversation from opportunity management to the full customer and product 360: from initial deal through NPI, operations, upsell, and cross-sell. This was territory Microsoft couldn't compete on
My Framework for Competitive Objections
Every competitor has talking points. The question is whether you play defense or change the game. My approach:
Challenge the premise
Don't accept every objection at face value. Understand whether it's a real requirement or a competitor-planted talking point.
Show, don't tell
Build it into the demo. Prove parity live instead of promising it on a roadmap slide.
Expand the frame
Go beyond the RFP. Show the customer what's possible that they haven't even asked for yet. Move the conversation to where the competitor can't follow.
Leverage the ecosystem
AppExchange partners, integrations, customer references — use the full platform story, not just the core product.
